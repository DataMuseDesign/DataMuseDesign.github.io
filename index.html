Understanding Academic Success Across the Boston Area

The American public school system has been increasingly focused on attempting to encourage all students to succeed academically. This goal is demonstrated by famous policies like "No Child Left Behind", the proliferation of charter schools, and the amount of energy dedicated to standardized testing. But have these attempts to level the playing field really worked to lift all students and school districts to a satisfactory level of performance?

We looked at data from the MA department of Education, profiling over 400 districts across the state and reporting on the resources allocated, student characteristics, and student performance.

Economic disadvantage reduces performance in school
and has lasting effects after leaving the public school system

Even with current measures to address disparities in academic performance, economically disadvantaged students do not perform at the same level as their wealthier counterparts. This shows up with their ability to meet academic standards during their time in the public school system and limits their future career options.

Districts with more resources don't perform better...
but districts with poorer students and low teacher retention perform far worse.

If Federal and state efforts are succeeding, you would expect all districts to have similar overall performance, or at least for districts with more resources (money, teachers, etc.) to be outpacing other districts. Unfortunately, this is not the case. The above chart illustrates how little correlation exists between district resources and the academic performance of students in larger districts (4.5k+ students) across the greater Boston area.

A variety of other factors affect academic performance in Massachusetts

In addition to our previously discovered correlations with district demographics and resources, other confounding factors can play a role in student success. In the final visualization, you can explore the relationship between a variety of factors against the success traits elaborated on previously. Between two different views, you can either compare all the districts as a whole or select specific districts to be placed in a scatter plot by clicking on specific nodes.



At least in Massachusetts, historic initiatives have not allowed all students to flourish academically. Something needs to be done, but simply throwing more money at existing programs is unlikely to help – it's time to be more creative.

And one thing is clear – the solution needs to address districts with the worst teacher retention and understand what type of support economically disadvantaged students are lacking.
